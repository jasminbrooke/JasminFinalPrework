Birds of Long Island

Long Island, New York is an island that stretches 118 miles from New York City to Montauk Point. It is also home to various types of birds! From our suburban towns to our beautiful beaches, there are so many opportunities to see different species of all shapes, sizes and colors. Here are some common birds you may see in your backyard or in your local park!


Northern Cardinal

The Northern Cardinal is one of the most popular birds in the eastern United States. It lives in forests, thickets and suburban gardens. The male is bright crimson red and the female is fawn (light brown). They eat grains, seeds and insects. The male will feed the female as part of the courtship rituals. They love sunflower seeds!


Blue Jay

The Blue Jay is a corvid native to eastern North America. It is common to see in residential areas. They eat seeds, nuts, insects and soft fruits. Male and female are similar in size and plumage. Although they are quite beautiful, they can be considered noisy and aggressive birds, and may chase smaller songbirds away from your backyard feeder!


Downy Woodpecker

The Downy Woodpecker lives in most forests of the United States. It is one of the smallest woodpeckers. They mainly eat insects by foraging in the bark of trees. The male and female appear similar, but the male has a red patch on the back of it's head. A common visitor to backyard bird feeders, attract them with specialized suet cakes.


Red-tailed Hawk

Adaptable and opporuntistic, The Red-Tailed Hawk can be seen throughout North America and Canada. They mostly eat small mammals, such as squirrels and rabbits, but will also eat other birds and reptiles. The females can be up to a quarter larger than the males. They can often be seen roosting on tall trees near highways.


Hooded Merganser

The Hooded Merganser is a type of water fowl that lives on lakes and ponds throughout North America. They mostly eat fish and hunt by sight by diving underwater. They display a high level of sexual dimorphism during the breeding season. The females are mostly brown with a reddish crest. The males have black and white plumage on their wings, neck and head and yellow eyes. Look for them at your local duck pond, along with Mallards and Wood Ducks. Remember, do not feed bread to the ducks!
